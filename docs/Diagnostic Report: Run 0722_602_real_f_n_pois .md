
## Diagnostic Report: Run 0722_602_real_f_n_pois — `kalman_update_activity` WARN

### What was flagged
Four locations (indices 58, 61, 68, 94) show a mean Kalman "update ratio" roughly 2–2.5x higher than the population of locations (0.58–0.62 vs. an overall mean of 0.24, with most locations clustered in 0.09–0.36), triggering a >3 SD outlier flag on the per-location mean.

### Investigation
I pulled the day-by-day update-ratio series for two of the four flagged locations (Northwest Territories, idx 58; Nunavut, idx 61) and the per-location ensemble collapse ratios for all 96 locations.

Key observations:
1. **Identity of the flagged locations**: idx 58 = Northwest Territories (CA), 61 = Nunavut (CA), 68 = Prince Edward Island (CA), 94 = Yukon (CA). These are the four smallest-population jurisdictions in the entire location set (three Canadian territories plus PEI), all with very low absolute case counts relative to every other location in the panel.
2. **Collapse ratios corroborate a location-specific pattern, not a random fluke**: these same four locations have by far the highest `alpha_collapse_ratio` (0.96–1.06, vs. a typical range of ~0.2–0.7 elsewhere) and elevated `beta_collapse_ratio` (0.40–1.04, vs. typical ~0.25–0.65). This means the parameter ensembles for alpha and beta in these locations are spending most of the run collapsed to near-zero spread — a self-reinforcing state where there is little ensemble spread left for the filter to meaningfully update.
3. **Temporal pattern**: the update-ratio time series for both locations shows a fast rise from near-zero (first ~2 weeks, plausibly burn-in) up to a sustained plateau around 0.5–0.8 for most of the run — this is a persistent regime, not a one-off spike from a single anomalous day. Nunavut additionally shows a distinct ~30-day period where the ratio drops sharply (down to 0.06–0.3) before recovering, consistent with a stretch of very sparse/zero case reports rather than a data artifact confined to one day.
4. Data completeness is not the driver: `frac_valid_days` is 0.986, essentially the same as other locations, so this isn't simply a case of missing data days being counted incorrectly.

### Explanation
**Observed pattern (plain language):** The four flagged locations are the smallest-population areas in the dataset. With low case counts, the Poisson observation model (this run uses a Poisson observation likelihood — "pois" in the run name) has high relative noise at low counts, and the ensemble for these locations tends to collapse (parameters converge tightly across ensemble members) more than in other locations. Once collapsed, the filter's correction step behaves differently — the update-ratio metric for these locations sits persistently higher than peers, which the outlier check correctly flagged as a location-specific deviation from the rest of the system.

**Confidence: medium.** The correlation between "smallest-population locations," "highest collapse ratios," and "highest update-ratio outliers" is a coherent and plausible story, and it's supported by both the collapse-ratio cross-check and the persistent (not spiky) nature of the time series. However, I cannot fully confirm the causal mechanism (i.e., that sparse/low counts under the Poisson observation model are mechanically responsible for the elevated ratio metric) without seeing the raw case-count time series for these locations or the exact definition/formula used for "update ratio."

### Alternative explanations considered
- **A location-indexing or configuration bug affecting exactly these four locations** (e.g., wrong population scaling, wrong observation variance parameter). Ranked lower because: (a) it would be a substantial coincidence for a bug to affect precisely the four smallest-population jurisdictions in the panel, and (b) the collapse-ratio evidence is a fully independent metric that shows the same four locations as extreme, which is more consistent with a real epidemiological/statistical mechanism (data sparsity) than a wiring bug that would typically also disrupt other unrelated metrics.
- **A genuine, isolated data-reporting anomaly (e.g., stretch of zero-reporting or backlog dumps) specific to these jurisdictions**, independent of population size per se. This is plausible and partially supported by the ~30-day anomalous dip seen in Nunavut's series, but cannot be distinguished from the general "small population → sparse/noisy signal" story with the data pulled so far — the two explanations are not mutually exclusive and may both be occurring.
- **This being purely a calibration/threshold artifact** (i.e., the SD-based outlier threshold is oversensitive here and this is expected behavior for low-count locations) — cannot be ruled out. The values are not close to either the `over_aggressive_threshold` (0.05) or `under_updating_threshold` (0.98), so on an absolute basis none of these locations are in a clearly pathological state; they are only anomalous *relative to their peers*.
